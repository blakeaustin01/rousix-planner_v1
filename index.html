How it works

A clearer way to explain surplus-based planning.

The language here is direct on purpose. The person should not need a finance degree to understand the idea.

1. Clear goal

The person chooses one thing they want to own: a car, truck, home, computer, business asset, or piece of equipment. The app turns that goal into a number.

2. Goal pathway

The planner shows a direct path toward the goal. It asks: “How much can you start with, how much can you add each month, and how much time do we have?”

3. Rousix infrastructure first

The Rousix machine is handled first. If the starting contribution does not cover the machine, the remaining machine cost is paid from the monthly contribution before the rest of the plan can support the goal.

4. Network effect, stated simply

If only a few people contribute small amounts, the system has less power. If more people apply, contribute, and purchase infrastructure, the network may become more useful. That is why the app shows 5x, 10x, 15x, and 40x examples — but they remain examples, not promises.

5. Careful expectations

Rousix can help clarify your goal, answer concerns, explain the onboarding steps, and help you decide whether to start small or go big. The honest message is simple: start with what you can afford to risk, build trust in the process, then increase only if the path makes sense.

Get started

Begin with a small step.

The simplest real-world flow is a $1 planning deposit, a $5+ starting contribution, and a $1+ monthly contribution. Bigger plans can come later.

Simple onboarding flow

  1. Enter personal credentials. Name, phone, email, and basic contact information.
  2. Choose your goal. Vehicle, home, equipment, business, workstation, or other.
  3. Enter the price. Keep the term simple: price.
  4. Enter contribution amounts. Starting contribution and monthly contribution.
  5. Review the roadmap. Print it, copy it, and talk through the risk.
  6. Start small or go big. Begin with a small test or move forward with a larger plan only after the person understands the risk.
